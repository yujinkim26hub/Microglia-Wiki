# Shimizu & Prinz (2025) — Microglia across evolution: from conserved origins to functional divergence

> [!note] Source note
> This is a **review article** (not primary research) and is **open access** ([PMC12660708](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12660708/)); the abstract and section structure were accessed directly. Individual review figures are not enumerated here in detail.

> [!abstract] One-line takeaway
> A comparative review arguing that [[microglia]] share conserved yolk-sac origins and core molecular signatures across vertebrates, yet diverge markedly in morphology, gene expression, and disease responses — divergence that must be accounted for when translating across model organisms.

## 1. Citation
Shimizu T, Prinz M. **Microglia across evolution: from conserved origins to functional divergence.** *Cellular & Molecular Immunology.* 2025 Dec;22(12):1533–1548. Epub 2025 Nov 21. doi:[10.1038/s41423-025-01368-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41423-025-01368-6). PMID: [41272275](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41272275/); PMCID: PMC12660708.

## 2. Core question
Which aspects of microglial biology are **conserved** across vertebrate evolution and which are **species-divergent**, and what does this mean for translating mechanistic findings across model organisms?

## 3. Prior studies
Decades of rodent work plus emerging zebrafish, nonhuman-primate, and human single-cell data have characterized microglial ontogeny, homeostasis, and disease responses — but largely within individual species.

## 4. Limitations of prior studies
Heavy reliance on rodent models, with cross-species differences (e.g., higher baseline immune gene expression and greater heterogeneity in primate/human microglia) often under-appreciated, risking mistranslation to human disease.

## 5. What this study did
The authors integrate findings across zebrafish, rodents, nonhuman primates, and humans to delineate conserved vs. divergent microglial features along five axes: ontogeny, maintenance, molecular signature, morphology, and functional roles/pathology.

## 6. Main results of this study
*(As a review, these are synthesized conclusions, not new experiments.)*
- **Ontogeny:** yolk-sac origin and core regulators (**PU.1/SPI1, IRF8**) and cues (**[[CSF1R]], TGF-β**) are conserved across mammals, birds, fish; microglia are a **vertebrate-specific innovation** (absent as bona fide cells in *Drosophila*/*C. elegans*).
- **Maintenance:** self-renewal via local proliferation independent of peripheral monocytes; turnover scales with lifespan (~28%/year in humans vs. rapid in zebrafish); continued PU.1/[[CSF1R]] dependence; strong regeneration after depletion.
- **Molecular signature:** conserved homeostatic genes (*P2RY12*, *TMEM119*, *HEXB*, *SALL1*, *CX3CR1*, *FCRLS*); [[disease-associated microglia|disease-associated]] programs are **partly conserved, partly species-specific**; primate/human microglia show higher baseline immune-gene expression and greater [[transcriptomics|transcriptional]] heterogeneity than mice.
- **Morphology:** ramified surveillance state conserved; branching complexity and amoeboid [[microglia activation|activation]] dynamics vary by species and are **not** linearly tied to brain size.
- **Function/pathology:** conserved roles (surveillance, debris clearance, synaptic pruning, myelin QC, neurogenesis support); divergent pathology — mammals show chronic inflammation impeding repair while zebrafish show transient, regeneration-permitting activation; in [[Alzheimer's disease]], a **biphasic** microglial role (early [[TREM2]]-mediated neuroprotection → later complement-mediated synapse loss).

## 7. Limitations of this study
As a narrative review: not systematic; cross-species data are uneven (rodent-heavy); molecular comparisons are constrained by differing platforms/annotations across species and studies.

## 8. Methods
Integrative/narrative literature review across vertebrate species (zebrafish, rodents, nonhuman primates, humans), organized along five thematic axes.

## 9. Key figures
Schematic/summary figures accompany the five themes (ontogeny, maintenance, molecular signature, morphology, function/pathology); individual figure-by-figure detail not enumerated from the source.

## 10. Main findings
- Microglial **origins and core identity are deeply conserved**; **responses and heterogeneity diverge** with lifespan, regenerative capacity, and immune architecture.
- Rodent models incompletely capture human microglia; comparative and naturally-diseased models (aged primates, dogs, cats) add value.
- Evolutionary context is essential for designing **microglia-targeted therapies** for human neurological disease.
